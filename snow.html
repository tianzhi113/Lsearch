Snow  
Cold air forms an invisible "sloping staircase" that causes water vapour to slide into the upper atmosphere. Cold and warm air masses meet to form a front. Warm, light air rich in water vapour slides or is lifted above the cold, dry air, causing the vapour to rise and form thick clouds. The water vapour is lifted into clouds and rain on the windward slope. A warm and moist air flow moving horizontally from an area rich in water vapor is forced to "climb" up the hillside to a higher ground if it is blocked by terrain such as mountains, hills, or plateaus in its direction of travel, resulting in clouds and rain on the windward slope.

Forming Reason
Heavy snow can seriously affect the safety of trains. Snow and freezing can obstruct the train driver's line of sight, reduce the lookout distance, and increase uncertainty and risk during the journey. In addition, snow accumulation can also accumulate on the tracks, affecting the traction and braking effectiveness of the train, increasing the risk of safety accidents such as derailment. In heavy snowstorms, the railway catenary may be frozen and the insulation may fail or short circuit, which in turn will affect the power supply of the trains. Ice formation on the catenary also increases the risk of arcing while the train is running, can damage the equipment, and requires speed limits to ensure safety. Prolonged blizzards and snow cover can cause damage to rail infrastructure. Snow accumulation can wash away the roadbed and cause damage to the roadbed; At the same time, the snow and ice inside the turnout can affect the normal operation of the turnout, increasing the risk of failure. Heavy snowfall can cause trains to be delayed or suspended, affecting the normal operation of the railway. Rail authorities need to adjust train schedules to meet safety requirements, but this can cause inconvenience to passengers and can cause pressure on passenger service.
