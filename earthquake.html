Earthquake   
The main cause of earthquakes is the squeezing and collision between plates on the earth, resulting in dislocation and rupture at the edge of the plate and within the plate. The place where the earthquake starts is called the epicenter, and the ground directly above the epicenter is called the epicenter. The most severe ground vibration in a destructive earthquake is called the extreme seismic zone, which is often the area where the epicenter is located. Earthquakes often cause serious casualties, which can cause fires, floods, toxic gas leaks, the spread of bacteria and radioactive materials, and may also cause secondary disasters such as tsunamis, landslides, collapses, and ground fissures.

Forming Reason
Earthquakes can trigger crustal movements and fractures, leading to land subsidence, foundation damage, bridge collapse, etc., which directly affect the capacity of railway lines. These geological changes can cause trains to not function properly or run at limited speeds, significantly delaying transit times and potentially disrupting rail transport altogether. The strong vibration of the earthquake will cause damage to the facilities and equipment of the railway, such as signal equipment, power supply system, communication equipment, etc., and the damage of these equipment will seriously affect the safety and efficiency of train operation. Earthquakes can also cause the collapse or severe damage of buildings such as stations and dispatch centers, further affecting the normal operation of rail transportation. After an earthquake, the risk of geological disasters such as geological landslides, mudslides, and landslides will increase significantly, and these disasters can directly threaten railway lines and nearby facilities, making train operation dangerous.
