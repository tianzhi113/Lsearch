Hail    
In hail clouds, updrafts are volatile, sometimes strong and sometimes weak. When the updraft is stronger, it brings the lower water droplets of the cloud to the middle and upper layers of the cloud, and the water droplets quickly cool and solidify into Xiaoice crystals. During the descent process, the Xiaoice crystal collided with supercooled water droplets, and then froze into an opaque ice core on Xiaoice crystal, which formed a hail embryo. Due to the drastic changes in the air flow in the hail cloud, the hail embryo also rolls up and down in the air again and again, attaching more supercooled water droplets, like a snowball, rolling bigger and bigger. Once it rolls into a large hail, when the updraft in the clouds cannot support it, it plummets and falls from the sky, becoming what the common people call "hail". The strong updraft not only transports sufficient water vapor to the hail cloud, but also supports the growth of hail particles in the cloud, making it grow to a considerable size before landing.

Forming Reason
Large hailstorms, with great impact and destructive power. When they directly hit the railway tracks, roadbeds, bridges and other infrastructure, they may cause local damage, such as track deformation, roadbed collapse, bridge structure damage, etc., which in turn affects the normal operation and safety of the railway. Electrified railways, such as high-speed rail, rely heavily on grid power for power. Hail weather may cause damage to power grid lines, such as broken lines, short circuits, etc., which will affect the power supply of trains and even cause train outages or failures. For trains transporting goods, hail weather can cause damage or loss of goods, resulting in economic losses for cargo owners. Hail weather may exacerbate soil erosion along the railway line and cause damage to the ecological environment. Hail can also cause damage to vegetation along the railway line, affecting the ecological balance and aesthetic landscape.
