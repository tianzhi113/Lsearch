   Wind     
Wind is a natural phenomenon on Earth, and it is caused by the heat of solar radiation. Sunlight shines on the earth's surface, causing the surface temperature to rise, and the air on the surface of the earth expands lightly and rises due to heating. When the hot air rises, the cold air at a low temperature flows in laterally, and the rising air cools and becomes heavier and falls, and the air heats up to rise due to the high surface temperature, and the flow of this air generates wind. And the ground is heated unevenly, causing the air to rise and sink in motion. This vertical movement of air creates a difference in air pressure on the same horizontal plane. I. Under the action of this force, the atmosphere moves horizontally from the high pressure area to the low pressure area, so that the wind is formed. It can be seen that the horizontal pressure gradient force is the direct cause of the formation of wind.

Forming Reason
Wind erodes the shoulder, resulting in a reduction in the width of the roadbed and exposure of the sleepers or track slabs, which in turn reduces the stability of the line. Strong winds and sand are also easy to cause sand accumulation on the track bed, which makes the track structure arch upward, affecting the smoothness of the line, and then threatening the driving speed and operation safety of high-speed trains. The debris carried by strong winds is easy to block the bridge and culvert, resulting in poor flood drainage and sediment discharge, and the bridge and culvert lose the efficiency of flood drainage and sand diversion. In addition, high winds can cause intense abrasion on the bridge structure, shortening its service life. When the lateral wind is large, it will produce a large lateral force on the body of the high-speed train, which will lead to the derailment or even overturning of the high-speed train in serious cases. This risk is particularly prominent in special environments (such as oversized bridges, high embankments, viaducts, passes, and canyon areas), because these places are prone to the production of the gorge effect and the speed increase effect, which exacerbates the impact of strong winds on trains. Strong winds may also cause damage to the external power supply equipment of the high-speed railway, such as scraping the catenary wires or even knocking down the catenary pillars, causing the train to lose power. When strong winds occur along high-speed railways near mountains or through mountains, secondary disasters such as landslides and mudslides may occur. These disasters will cause the accumulation of foreign objects along the railway line, and even bury the railway tracks and buildings, seriously threatening the safety of train operation, causing accidents such as train operation interruption and subversion.
