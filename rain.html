Rain 
Cold air forms an invisible "sloping staircase" that causes water vapour to slide into the upper atmosphere. Cold and warm air masses meet to form a front. Warm, light air rich in water vapour slides or is lifted above the cold, dry air, causing the vapour to rise and form thick clouds. The water vapour is lifted into clouds and rain on the windward slope. A warm and moist air flow moving horizontally from an area rich in water vapor is forced to "climb" up the hillside to a higher ground if it is blocked by terrain such as mountains, hills, or plateaus in its direction of travel, resulting in clouds and rain on the windward slope.

Heavy rain may also cause the railway line to be flooded, and the roadbed and the surrounding rock and soil are prone to landslides, debris flows and other geological disasters after water absorption, which directly threatens the safety of the train. To ensure safety, the railway authorities may take measures to reduce the speed of operation or suspend operations. Strong winds may blow tree branches, plastic bags and other foreign objects onto railway catenary equipment, causing short circuits or faults in the catenary, affecting power supply, and then affecting the operation of electrically driven trains. Floods caused by heavy rains can wash away railway embankments, causing damage to the roadbed, deformation of tracks, and even landslides, mudslides and other disasters. At the same time, flooding can also wash away bridges, culverts and other infrastructure, which can have a serious impact on the overall operation of the railway. Due to line blockades and equipment failures caused by heavy rains, the railway authorities may need to adjust the train operation plan, including train suspension and detour operation, to ensure the safety of the train. This will lead to train delays, stoppages, etc., causing inconvenience to passengers.
