Fog  
When the air temperature drops below the dew point, the water vapor in the air condenses into small water droplets or ice crystals, which forms a fog. This usually occurs at night or early in the morning, when the ground is in contact with the air, causing the air temperature to drop. Weak winds at night or early in the morning are the best times for fog to form.

Forming Reason
In foggy weather, visibility is significantly reduced by moisture and tiny particles in the air. When the visibility distance is less than the safe coasting distance required for train braking, the risk of accidents such as rear-end collision and derailment of the train increases. This risk is especially pronounced on high-speed railways. Heavy fog restricts the line of sight of train drivers, making it difficult for them to accurately judge the road conditions and signals ahead, increasing the uncertainty and difficulty of driving. Foggy weather can also cause fog flashes. Fog flash refers to the fact that in strong foggy weather, due to the large water vapor content in the air and the possibility of containing more pollutants, the wires are "polluted", which increases the conductivity of the foggy air sharply, and then causes faults such as short circuit and tripping of transmission lines. In extreme foggy weather, if visibility is extremely low and there is a serious safety hazard, the railway authorities may take measures such as suspending some trains or extending the interval between departures to ensure safety. This will directly lead to train stoppages or delays, affecting passengers' travel plans.
Railroad on foggy days

Poor line of sight: Poor visibility and slippery roads in foggy days prevent train drivers from seeing the railway signals ahead, including locomotive headlights and platform lights, during fogging, increasing the likelihood of train collisions. Signal interruption: The train disappears due to the power supply of the electric locomotive catenary in foggy days, and the signal cannot be displayed normally, which seriously affects the normal operation.

Cross-railroad tracks shrouded in fog

When there is no fork in the section, the turnout needs to be manually pulled because the turnout cannot be rotated. This requires a lot of manpower and time. Increased braking distances for trains: Due to poor visibility in foggy weather, trains must slow down or stop so that drivers can see the signals ahead. This increases the braking distance of the train and sometimes even requires a temporary stop.
